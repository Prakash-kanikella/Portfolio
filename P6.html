Reducing Carbon Footprint in Chemical Plants

A Techno-Economic Analysis of Renewable Energy Integration

Project Abstract

The chemical industry is a major contributor to global carbon emissions due to its heavy reliance on fossil fuels. This project investigates the integration of renewable energy sources—such as solar, wind, and biomass—to significantly reduce the carbon footprint of mid-scale chemical plants.

An engineered model was developed to project a 12-15% reduction in overall carbon emissions by integrating a solar-thermal hybrid system, validated through a comprehensive techno-economic analysis and lifecycle assessment.

Core Analysis & Methodology

Renewable Integration Model

Engineered a model to simulate the integration of various renewable technologies (solar, wind, biomass, green hydrogen) into existing chemical plant energy systems.

Techno-Economic Analysis

Evaluated the financial viability, including initial capital costs, operational savings, and return on investment for adopting solar-thermal hybrid systems.

Lifecycle Assessment (LCA)

Conducted an LCA to quantify the environmental benefits, from raw material extraction for panels to decommissioning, ensuring a holistic view of the carbon reduction.

Validation

The analysis concluded that integrating solar-thermal hybrid systems is not only environmentally beneficial but also financially viable for mid-scale chemical plants, presenting a strong business case for decarbonization.

Conclusion

This project provides a clear, data-driven framework for the chemical industry's transition toward greener operations. It successfully demonstrates that strategic renewable integration is a powerful and viable solution for achieving significant and measurable reductions in carbon emissions.
